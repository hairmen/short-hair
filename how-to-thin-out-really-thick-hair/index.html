How to Thin Out Really Thick Hair - Tips and Tricks

Thick hair is a blessing and a curse. While it can be voluminous and luxurious, it can also be difficult to manage and style. One way to make thick hair more manageable is by thinning it out. In this article, we will discuss how to thin out really thick hair, step by step.

How to Thin Out Really Thick Hair

There are several ways to thin out really thick hair, including:

  • Layering
  • Razor cutting
  • Thinning shears
  • Texturizing

Each method has its own pros and cons, and the best method for you will depend on your hair type and personal preferences.

Layering

Layering involves cutting the hair at different lengths to create texture and movement. This is a great option for thick hair, as it can help to remove some of the bulk while also adding depth and dimension.

Razor Cutting

Razor cutting involves using a razor blade to thin out the hair. This method is best for those with straight or wavy hair, as it can create a choppy, edgy look. However, it should be avoided if you have curly hair, as it can cause frizz and damage.

Thinning Shears

Thinning shears are specifically designed to remove bulk from the hair. They have teeth that are spaced apart to allow some strands to pass through while cutting others. This method is best for those with thick, curly hair, as it can help to reduce the volume and make the curls more defined.

Texturizing

Texturizing involves cutting the hair at different angles to create texture and movement. This method is best for those with straight or wavy hair, as it can add volume and dimension without removing too much length.

Step-by-Step Guide

Here is a step-by-step guide on how to thin out really thick hair:

  1. Start by washing and conditioning your hair.
  2. Towel dry your hair until it is damp, but not dripping wet.
  3. Section your hair into four parts: top, sides, and back.
  4. Starting with the back section, take a small section of hair and comb it straight.
  5. Hold the section of hair between your fingers and cut into it using the desired method (layering, razor cutting, thinning shears, or texturizing).
  6. Repeat this process on all sections of your hair, making sure to blend the cuts together.
  7. Once you have finished cutting your hair, style it as desired.

Tips for Thinning Out Thick Hair

Here are some tips to keep in mind when thinning out thick hair:

  • Start with small sections of hair and work your way up to larger sections.
  • Use sharp scissors or shears to avoid damaging the hair.
  • Be careful not to cut too much hair at once, as this can result in an uneven cut.
  • Take breaks as needed to avoid hand fatigue.
  • Consult with a professional stylist if you are unsure about which method to use.

FAQs

Can thinning out my hair cause damage?

Thinning out your hair can cause damage if not done properly. It is important to use sharp scissors or shears and to avoid cutting too much hair at once.

Will thinning out my hair make it grow back thicker?

No, thinning out your hair will not make it grow back thicker. It will only remove some of the bulk and make it more manageable.

How often should I thin out my hair?

The frequency at which you should thin out your hair will depend on your hair type and personal preferences. It is generally recommended to do it every 6-12 months.

Hair Care for Thick Hair

Thick hair requires special care to keep it healthy and manageable. Here are some tips for caring for thick hair:

  • Use a gentle shampoo and conditioner that is specifically designed for thick hair.
  • Avoid using hot tools (such as flat irons and curling irons) on a daily basis, as this can cause damage.
  • Avoid brushing your hair when it is wet, as this can cause breakage.
  • Use a wide-tooth comb or pick to detangle your hair.
  • Avoid using heavy styling products that can weigh down your hair.

Thinning out really thick hair can be a great way to make it more manageable and easier to style. By following the tips and tricks in this article, you can achieve the perfect look for your hair type and personal preferences.
